Part I — What we're building

1. The vision: a venture-studio holding company

Not "Orectic plus a marketing wrapper." Something bigger and cleaner:

  • Orectic — the platform. The AI-native operating substrate every engagement runs on, proudly carried as "Powered by Orectic" on everything we ship. Isaiah's life's work becomes the durable moat of a studio, not a single founder's solo product.
  • Ovae — the operating company. The single front door that signs clients across verticals, installs the platform, runs the transformation, and owns the GTM, brand, and delivery motion. The engine.
  • The HoldCo — the compounding machine. Owns Orectic as flagship IP, owns the co-created vertical runbook moat, and — the real prize — can build an equity portfolio of the companies it transforms (equity-for-services and exit-tails alongside cash). We don't just bill installs; we accumulate ownership in the businesses we make more valuable.

This is the model the one-pager already points at: the runbook is at once the client lock-in, the productization engine, and the owned IP that re-rates the firm from a services multiple to a platform multiple. The studio frame is what makes that triple payoff structural instead of incidental.

2. The prize — and the one assumption it rests on

A founder-owned $15–20M trades company yields ~$130–200K blended 3-year ACV. 10–12 core clients on retainer + build clears ~$1M in firm billings (reachable for two founders in 18–24 months). The 5-year target is $8–15M firm revenue at 35–40% EBITDA, 50%+ recurring → a ~$100M+ enterprise value at a platform multiple — before any equity stakes the HoldCo takes in the clients it transforms.

The honest caveat: that's a target conditioned on one number, not a forecast. The model lives or dies on audit-to-build conversion — what share of $17,500 Land engagements convert up into $90K builds and $175–225K embeds. At a healthy 30–40% it's exceptional land economics; below that, the audit is just an expensive lead magnet. We don't know the real rate yet — which is exactly why we instrument it from client #1, and why the first 90 days matter.

Naming the prize — and its one load-bearing assumption — first matters because, against a $100M+ build, the questions we have to settle between us are small by comparison. The fastest way there is to make the structure fair quickly, then go prove the conversion number together.

3. Why this is the best path for both of us

Each of us could take a separate route — CT could build Ovae on another platform with other technical partners; Isaiah could keep selling Orectic solo or find a different GTM partner. Both are real. But the honest read is that the combined path is stronger for both:

  • Two engines, not one. A great platform with no distribution is a feature list; distribution with no platform is a pitch deck. Together we have both — deep product plus a proven go-to-market and capital engine.
  • The math favors together. Apart, the realistic outcomes are modest; together — raising capital, landing clients, compounding a runbook moat and an equity portfolio — the target is an order of magnitude larger. It's less "what does each give up" and more "which bet would each of us rather hold."
  • Neither of us can lose what we brought. Because of reversion (Orectic returns to Isaiah on a failure) and its mirror (Ovae stays with CT), each of us keeps our half if it ever unwinds. That mutual exit-ramp is exactly what makes it safe enough to fully commit now.

Where the moat actually is

Worth being clear-eyed about together, because it shapes everything we build. In 2026, the platform itself isn't the moat — for anyone. The base Orectic runs on (Nous Research's Hermes, MIT-licensed) is one of the most popular open-source agent frameworks in the world, and Microsoft is shipping free governance tooling around the same problem. CT knows this first-hand — he built his own AI operating system on the same base to run OPUS. Orectic is the more advanced system today, but a layer on a commoditizing base, however good, isn't by itself a durable moat.

That's not a knock; it's the market. What actually defends an AI company now is well-documented: deep vertical expertise, becoming the system of record in a niche, proprietary workflows and data, proven client outcomes, and speed of capture — in a space that's hot, crowded, and full of "agent-washing" (by one industry count, only ~130 of thousands of self-described agent vendors are the real thing).

So the moat we're actually building is the combination only the two of us produce: Orectic kept on the cutting edge by Isaiah — with the engineering investment, likely including a dedicated CTO, that staying ahead requires — wrapped in chosen verticals, co-created runbooks, proven installs, a real go-to-market engine, and the holding company that compounds it. The platform is the foundation; the defensibility is what we build on top of it, fast. That's exactly why doing this together, quickly, is the winning move — and why most of the value is created forward.

Part II — The framework

Both contributions are real. The only open question is how to structure ownership so neither the pre-existing platform nor the forward go-to-market value is mispriced.

A fair map separates four things: what each of us already brings that's real, what we created together, and what still has to be built. Separating them matters because the structure should reward proven contribution and earned-forward contribution differently — and being honest about which is which is what keeps the split fair to both of us.

Not one of us being "more" — the two halves of a fundable company locking together.

Recognizing the head start — options, not a demand

8. The first 90 days — our opening sprint

A paid, shared first sprint with a clear decision at the end — not an audition.

  • Day 0 — clean paper before public selling: mutual NDA; CT assigns/licenses the Ovae name, site, research, and configurator into the entity; a role + decision-rights memo; conflict disclosure; a claim-control policy; and a contribution-ledger template. The contracting entity is decided up front.
  • A paid floor during the sprint — a modest retainer or commission against collected revenue — so the window is covered if deals slip.
  • A pre-agreed milestone: the day-90 grant and its triggers are written now — e.g., first $17,500 Land closed + ≥$100–150K contracted → first equity tranche vests, 4-yr / 1-yr-cliff.
  • Days 1–30: operating rhythm, role clarity, funnel; build the buyer ledger.
  • Days 31–60: first vertical campaign; first paid engagement(s).
  • Days 61–90: turn delivery into a repeatable playbook; ratify the standing structure (§14).

9. Three ways to phase the economics

All keep the same architecture (HoldCo + reversion + vesting); they differ in how much equity moves up front versus on proof.

  • A — Conservative: the sprint is paid via commission + a small advisory option; HoldCo equity enters at day 90 on metrics, then full vesting and reversion. Most proof first; most upside deferred.
  • B — Faster signal (our suggestion): paid floor; enter the 55/45 HoldCo at signing, with the first tranche vesting on first collected revenue and the rest milestone-gated. Equity tracks real cash; the payout is defined up front for both.
  • C — Strong start: paid floor + full 55/45 granted at signing, 4-yr/1-yr-cliff, accelerating on financing/valuation milestones and fully on a sale. Highest trust and speed.

S1. Options 1 & 2 at exit — running the numbers (choose one, not both)

  • Option 2 — a contribution preference + an even-er split. Isaiah takes an agreed amount ($1.5–3M) off the top on any exit, and the residual equity moves toward ~50/50 to reflect forward contribution. The preference does the head-start recognition; the equity reflects what's built from here.

S2. How the client-equity portfolio could work

The "equity in the companies we transform" idea needs real mechanics to be more than a slogan. The typical client (founder-owned $15–20M trades company, 3–5 years from a sale) is usually an S-corp or LLC, so direct equity is awkward. Practical instruments, easiest first:

  • Exit-tail / transaction right (best fit). A small share of the owner's eventual sale price. They're already heading toward an exit, so it aligns with their motive and needs no cap-table entry.
  • Synthetic equity / margin share. A contractual share of the incremental margin the transformation unlocks, for a fixed term — easy to accept, no dilution.
  • Sweat warrant. A warrant to buy a set stake at a set strike, earned on delivery — for the cleanest-books clients open to real equity.
  • An SPV under the HoldCo to hold the stakes, warrants, and tails as a portfolio.

Guardrails that keep it from distracting from the core business: cash first (Land + build + retainer fund operations); equity/tail is an optional premium track that trades cash for upside, never mandatory; offered only to best-fit clients; start synthetic before real equity. Counsel reviews concentration risk, S-corp constraints, and securities treatment.
