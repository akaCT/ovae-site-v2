1. The premise — why this is worth it for both of us

The partnership rests on a clean division of labor neither of us can easily replicate alone:

Isaiah builds the platform. CT builds the machine that fills it with customers and runs them — brand, sales engine, delivery team, events, relationships, onboarding. Neither half gets built fast, alone.

One insight drives everything below: the durable, acquirable asset — the co-created runbooks and playbooks, and the position of being the system each client runs on — is built going forward. Isaiah brought the platform and a real head start. That asset is built from the clients Ovae brings in and operates. (Raw customer data on its own doesn't compound into a moat; the codified playbooks and the operating position do.) This isn't "CT rents Isaiah's IP." It's Isaiah brings the engine and the head start; Ovae brings the distribution that builds the moat going forward; both founders own what compounds.

That's the alignment. Building solo, Isaiah can keep improving Orectic but stays capped without brand, GTM, a delivery layer, or a way out of services, which he's said he wants. For CT, the realistic alternative to this partnership is building the same platform himself or standing up an engineering team to do it: the same bet, with less of a head start. Partnering is the faster, more aligned path for both of us. We marry product and distribution and capture the combined value. Isaiah keeps building the product; Ovae is the single front door; both own what compounds.

2. The structure — and the options we considered

Three structures were on the table:

  • ① Orectic is the company; Ovae is its GTM brand. Cleanest for Isaiah's value capture, but CT doesn't own a real company — closer to the Opus dynamic.
  • ② Two independent companies + a license. Each keeps their own entity — but it's the most fragile: channel conflict, IP outside the funded entity, a license to police. This is roughly where we started, and it's the source of the unease.
  • ③ HoldCo over both ✅ recommended. A parent owns Orectic (product) and Ovae (GTM) as operating brands. It's the only structure that delivers the stated goal: a valuable company and a valuable asset, where an acquirer buys all of the above.

The one refinement the experts insisted on: in a HoldCo, the IP and co-created data/runbooks must sit at the parent, not inside Isaiah's Orectic sub. If the moat data lives only in Orectic, a split cuts CT out of the value he helped build — and an acquirer of the whole is two layers removed from the asset. Parent-level ownership fixes both.

3. The money

3a. Equity — 55 / 45, and the reasoning behind it

Recommended: 55% Isaiah / 45% CT of founder equity (plus the option pool below). This was pressure-tested across five independent expert lenses; three objective ones (founder-split frameworks, VC fundability, value-creation/M&A) converged at 52–55 Isaiah / 45–48 CT, and 55/45 sits at the Isaiah-favorable edge of that band. Here's the shared logic behind it:

  • Isaiah earns the majority for the head start. He built the product, already has a paying client, and has live inbound — that de-risks the biggest question any company faces ("will anyone actually pay for this?"). That's worth a real premium, and it's why he holds the larger share and the lead-founder seat.
  • Most of the value is built forward, together. The durable moat — the co-created runbooks and playbooks per vertical, and the position of being the system each client runs on — plus the scale, the brand, the pipeline, the capital, and the eventual exit are created after day one, largely through the go-to-market engine CT leads. CT brings years of building scalable businesses, fundraising, go-to-market, brand identity and voice, events, and strategy. A great platform with no distribution stays a boutique practice; distribution and operating depth are what turn it into a company. That forward half is roughly the 45%.
  • The base is open-source — so the moat isn't the code, it's what we build on top of it, together. That's not a knock on the product; it's the whole reason the partnership matters: the defensible, compounding asset is co-created, so both founders should own it.
  • We're expanding the pie, not dividing a fixed one. Solo, the realistic ceiling is a strong boutique. Together — product + distribution + capital — the outcome is an order of magnitude larger. 55% of that is worth far more than 100% of the solo path. That's the case, and it's true.

3b. Income now — salary, not a draw

  • Equal salaries — ~$25K/mo each. Funded from client revenue — start payroll once 2–3 clients are closed; don't pay ahead of revenue. Equity carries the contribution difference; equal monthly cash comp keeps the day-to-day dynamic clean.
  • Documented as W-2 compensation for a defined role, not a founder draw or advance. (Draws-that-become-loans-that-erase-equity-claims is exactly the Opus mechanism — avoid it by structure.)
  • Ring-fence a ~$30K/mo hiring pool before scaling founder pay. Build a profitable company; keep cash in to hire.
  • Post-profitability: founder pay can move to ~$30–35K/mo once monthly net-after-payroll clears ~$150K; profit distributions above that split pro-rata to equity, only when runway > 6 months.

3c. Capital — bootstrap first

Use the hot inbound to close 10–15 clients and build a year of platform-revenue data before raising. A seed today buys money at a ~$6–10M pre (15–25% dilution); the same raise after ~$1M ARR prices at ~$15–25M pre (10–15% dilution) — a multi-million-dollar founder-net-worth difference. Raise only when a specific need (a product team, expansion) exceeds what operations generate — not to "get going." We're already going.

3d. Earning more — base, profit, bonus, and a near-term cash path

Salary is just the base. Here's how we actually get paid — and how we responsibly pay ourselves more as the business wins:

  • Base salary (the target). Equal ~$25K/mo each, W-2, for a defined role, funded by client revenue. This is the steady-state target once 2–3 clients are closed; founders run leaner (~$15K/mo) during the initial ramp, then settle near ~$25K and ~$30K as ARR scales. Kept deliberately lean so cash stays in to hire and grow.
  • Profit distributions (the main "more" lever). Past a retained-cash buffer (≥6 months runway + the hiring pool), surplus profit is distributed pro-rata to equity (55/45), quarterly. It scales with the business: the better we do, the more we both take home — without inflating base.
  • Founder performance bonus. A modest annual bonus tied to clear targets (ARR or net-profit milestones), so results are rewarded while base stays lean (what investors want to see).
  • Near-term origination incentive (bootstrap phase only — capped, then sunsets). To reward fast cash generation in the first ~6 months, a small capped bonus on net-new logos CT personally closes (a flat amount per closed Embed, or a few % of first-year contract value, capped). It ends once the GTM team is hired — a kickstart, not a permanent founder commission.
  • Referral/partner fees (e.g., David's ~10%) go to the outside referrer as a cost of sale — founders don't take commission on those.
  • What we avoid: large permanent founder commissions. As owners we already hold the upside through equity; a standing commission double-dips, muddies the cap-table economics, and reads poorly to investors (same logic that killed the Orectic royalty).

How VC / PE / M&A see it. Pre-raise (where we are) there are no "VC rules" on our pay — it's a bootstrapped, profitable business, so we pay what it genuinely affords (base + distributions), and that's attractive: distributions from a real business signal a fundable company, not a science project. When we raise, comp resets to market and board approval — typically ~$175–225K and under ~15% of burn; big salaries on investor cash are a red flag, lean base + equity is the norm. PE/M&A buyers normalize owner comp — excess pay is an add-back in diligence, so reasonable pay doesn't hurt valuation; they price on growth and (for PE) normalized EBITDA.

The deposit-driven funnel front-loads cash. A paid audit plus 50% on signature means Krista ($175K) plus ~$300K of near pipeline puts ~$0.5–1M contracted within ~90 days; 2–3 Embeds cover both salaries plus the hiring pool with surplus left. The business funds its own delivery and first hires from day one.

4. Vesting & mutual protection

4a. Vesting — symmetric, milestone-accelerated, with a mutual cliff

Plain four-year vesting feels off if we raise big or exit in ~2 years. So we use a model that's time-based as the floor but accelerates on the milestones that matter — VC-friendly and exit-friendly. Both founders, same terms:

  • Floor (what investors expect to see): a 4-year schedule with a 12-month mutual cliff. Neither gets it all up front; if either walks in year one, they leave with little. This backstop keeps us both committed and reads clean in diligence.
  • Milestone accelerators (the milestone-driven part): a defined chunk of unvested equity vests on each priced financing round and on hitting valuation thresholds we set (e.g., first priced round, Series A / $X valuation). Grow fast, vest fast.
  • Full acceleration on a sale: a change of control fully vests both founders (founder single-trigger, or double-trigger with termination — investor-negotiable). This is the clause that answers "what if we exit in two years" — you're 100% vested at the exit, regardless of the calendar.
  • Why not pure milestone-only: if a milestone slips, milestone-only leaves a founder stranded under-vested (instability), and VCs dislike the incentive to force a round. The time-floor-plus-accelerators gives the milestone feel with none of that downside — it only ever speeds up, never strands.
  • Isaiah's head start can be recognized with modest vesting credit at close (e.g., 6–12 months), then the same go-forward schedule as CT.

(The milestone-linked tranche idea from earlier is gone — superseded by this. Symmetric vesting + the milestone accelerators + the §4b clean-break do its job: if CT underdelivers and we part, his unvested equity returns and Isaiah keeps Orectic.)

4b. Protections — symmetric, in the shareholders' agreement (not a handshake)

The clean break — each keeps their lane (this is the heart of it). The single thing that makes it safe enough for both of us to start now: if it ends, neither is stranded.

  • Orectic reverts to Isaiah. The product and IP he built return to him (contribution + dormant-license terms). He never loses the thing he built.
  • Ovae stays with CT — the brand, the go-to-market, the client relationships, and the team. And because the base is open-source, CT can rebuild the product layer with another technical partner. CT walks in with a real lane and a real alternative path, exactly as Isaiah does.
  • The co-created moat (data + runbooks) is shared on a split — each gets a perpetual license to the generalized playbooks; client relationships follow Ovae.
  • Both have somewhere to land. That mutual exit-ramp is what lets us commit fully and sleep at night.

For CT: - Equity (not commission); anti-dilution floor (can't be pushed below a set floor without his consent); equal governance (board 1-1-1 with one independent, no unilateral action); pro-rata rights; defined role with acceleration if removed without cause + a broad "good-leaver" clause. - Downside beyond equity: the new C-corp shields personal liability; Isaiah warrants the contributed Orectic IP is clean — properly-licensed open source, no infringement, no encumbrances — so CT isn't exposed to product-origin claims; the company carries D&O / E&O insurance from the first client.

For Isaiah: - Reversion of Orectic (above) — the core "it stays his." - Reverse-vesting / clawback if CT exits early; role definition with material-non-performance defined (this, plus CT's vesting and the clean break, replaces the milestone tranche). - Each founder warrants no undisclosed outside liabilities flow into the company. CT's prior company is a separate entity, cleanly walled off (a different business, contributed as labor and relationships rather than moved assets, so no successor liability). Brand control over the "Powered by Orectic" mark.

5. The IP & the moat — the thing to lock this week

Isaiah is right that the open-source base isn't the moat. The moat is the co-created runbooks and playbooks that compound per vertical — the "AI-native in 90 days" promise, proven and documented — plus becoming the system each client runs on. (Raw customer data on its own isn't the moat; the codified playbooks and the operating position are.) Here's how to protect it:

  • Trademark "Orectic" and "Ovae" (USPTO, Class 42) — file within 30 days; most durable, easiest to enforce.
  • Trade secrets — the runbooks, vertical playbooks, and data pipeline are protectable only if kept secret: NDAs on every contractor/employee + access controls. This is the billion-dollar asset; treat it that way.
  • Copyright — register Isaiah's core modifications for litigation standing.
  • Client contracts (the clause founders forget, and the one that matters most at exit): every MSA must grant the company a perpetual, royalty-free license to use de-identified, aggregated operational data, and must state that generalized runbooks/configurations derived from engagements are owned by the company (client keeps only their specific instantiation). Without this, clients later claim co-ownership of the moat and the story collapses in diligence.
  • Patents: not realistic here — spend the energy on trademarks, trade secrets, and contract terms.
  • Ownership principle: all runbooks + de-identified data + trained configs are HoldCo assets, licensed down to each brand. An acquirer buys the parent and gets everything; neither founder can be cut out of the asset they co-built.

6. Pre-mortem — how this fails (both sides), so we design against it

Most likely killer: drift. Hot inbound → first big client signed on a handshake → "we'll paper it later" → 6 good months → one small misalignment with no framework to resolve it → the partnership quietly stalls, with nothing signed. Governance isn't bureaucracy; it's what keeps a good relationship good under pressure.

Top failure modes & early warnings: 1. Isaiah goes solo after CT validates the GTM playbook. Warning: he starts taking discovery calls "just to understand the client." 2. Co-created moat becomes leverage against CT — clients' data/runbooks live in Isaiah's sub; on a split he has the asset and a direct line to the buyers. Fixed by §5 parent-level ownership. 3. Cliff-then-full-grant leaves no alignment (and no remedy) post-cliff. Fixed by §4a. 4. Services gravity — CT needs services revenue now; Isaiah wants out of services. Warning: Isaiah skips an onboarding call. Fixed by extracting Isaiah from delivery within ~60 days via the builder bench + productizing delivery (The Brain). 5. Outside pressure pulls either founder off the build or rushes a decision. Fixed by guaranteed salaries and clear roles, so neither of us negotiates under pressure. 6. Option-pool dilution without CT's vote; 50/50 deadlock with no tiebreaker. Fixed by §4b governance.

Steelman of "Isaiah just stays solo + pays a referral fee": with hot, free inbound, that's rational for him unless Ovae delivers four things a $5K/mo BD person can't: (1) pipeline 10× what he can close alone, validated in 90 days; (2) a defensible brand/category he owns; (3) events/community network effects; (4) a delivery layer that gets him out of services entirely. If we can't commit to those in writing with milestones, he should prefer solo — so the structure has to make Ovae the better choice for him, not just for CT.

7. Liquidity & the long game — co-equal and exponential

This is not an early sale. The path: bootstrap to ~$10M ARR, then a strategic round from strength (primary + secondary) at a high multiple — and keep compounding. At Isaiah's own ~25× reference for this space, $10M ARR ≈ $250M; more conservatively, a defensible AI company at $10M ARR runs ~$100–200M (≈$150M with clean metrics + >120% NRR).

Take money off the table without selling the company. A ~$20–25M round structured as ~$15M primary (~10% dilution) + ~$10M secondary lets each of us pull ~$5M+ now while keeping control — big check, small dilution, partial liquidity. That gives both of us meaningful liquidity now, while the bulk of the upside keeps compounding toward a much larger eventual outcome. Same curve for both. $60–80M is a milestone we blow past, not the ceiling.

If a whole-company sale ever does make sense, the HoldCo (product + data + runbooks + client book + team) is the clean "acquire all of the above" package — a choice we earn, not the plan.

8. Next steps — what to sign before the next client

Ranked by importance (per the legal + pre-mortem lenses):

  1. IP ownership + client data/runbook rights — MSA template with the data-license + runbook-ownership clauses (§5). Without this, the company builds its core asset without owning it, and the exit story breaks.
  2. Founder agreement + vesting (RSPAs, §4) — symmetric vesting + mutual cliff, defined roles, cure periods, the clean-break/walk-away terms, buyout-on-deadlock.
  3. Decision-authority matrix — who decides what unilaterally, what needs consensus, what happens on deadlock.
  4. CT income as a guaranteed salary (§3b), with named funding mechanics.
  5. Orectic contribution agreement + IP assignment — rolls Isaiah's build into the structure, credited, and assigns all past/future IP to the company.

30/90-day checklist: trademarks filed (≤30d) · NDAs + access controls on runbooks · contribution + IP assignment executed · client MSA template finalized with data clauses · copyright registration · SOC2 readiness gap-check. Don't take a paying client until items 1, 2, and 5 are signed.
