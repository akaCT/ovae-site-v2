Orectic — Contribution & Reversion Terms

The mechanism that makes "it stays his" real · For Isaiah & CT · May 2026

What this is. A plain-English term sheet for the one piece that has to feel airtight for Isaiah: he contributes Orectic into the company to build the upside together, but he is guaranteed never to lose the thing he built. This is a founders' alignment draft — not legal, tax, or accounting advice, and not binding. A startup attorney drafts the actual Contribution Agreement, the standby license, and the operating agreement; a few points below are explicitly flagged for counsel.

The idea in one paragraph

Isaiah contributes Orectic (the platform, the code, the brand, the IP he built) into the HoldCo in exchange for his founder equity. From day one it powers the whole company. But the deal is wired so that if the partnership ever fails or unwinds, Orectic returns to Isaiah automatically — and, symmetrically, Ovae (the brand, go-to-market, clients, and team) stays with CT. Each contributes their half to build the upside together; each keeps their half if it doesn't work out. Contribution for the upside; a clean, two-sided break for the downside. (See §5b for the mirror.)

1. What Isaiah contributes (and what he gets)
Contributes The Orectic platform: his original code and modifications, the architecture, the trademarks/name/domains, the product roadmap, and the IP he created — assigned to a dedicated Orectic subsidiary under the HoldCo (see §3 for why a sub).
Does NOT contribute The open-source base (Hermes/MIT and other OSS) — it isn't his to give; it stays open-source underneath. He contributes his work on top of it.
Gets His founder equity (the 55%) in the HoldCo — equity, not cash.
Tax treatment Structured as a tax-free contribution of property for equity (§351 if the HoldCo is a C-corp / §721 if an LLC). He receives stock/units, not cash, so no taxable event on contribution. (Confirm with counsel/CPA.)
Valuation The contribution is assigned a documented value (e.g., a defensible $250K–$500K for the platform + head start) to set his tax basis and paper the record. The equity split — not the valuation number — governs ownership.

2. Why it's contributed at all (not just licensed)

We considered keeping Orectic as Isaiah's separate company that the business merely licenses (the "two-companies" option). We're not doing that, because it's fragile: it puts the core IP outside the company investors fund and acquirers buy, creates a license to police, and risks channel conflict. Contributing Orectic in — with an ironclad reversion right — gives Isaiah the same "it's mine" protection without the fragility, and makes the company cleanly fundraiseable and acquirable. Best of both.

3. How it's housed (so reversion is clean)

  • The Orectic platform IP and brand live in their own subsidiary (Orectic LLC) under the HoldCo. This is deliberate: a dedicated sub is cleanly severable — on a trigger, the whole sub (or its assets) returns to Isaiah without untangling it from everything else.
  • People are still employed by the operating company, not the sub — one company, one team, one payroll (the sub is where the IP legally sits, not who works there).
  • The co-created moat — the vertical data + runbooks built with clients — is owned at the HoldCo parent, not the Orectic sub, because CT and the team co-build it. (Its treatment on a split is in §6 — this is the one genuinely shared asset.)

4. The reversion right (the core protection)

On a Trigger Event (§5), the following happens automatically, by pre-signed agreement:

  1. The Orectic sub — the platform, code, IP, trademarks, domains, roadmap — assigns back to Isaiah (or a new entity he designates), free of the HoldCo's claims.
  2. A standby exclusive license, dormant during normal operations, springs live the instant a trigger fires — so even if the mechanical transfer takes time, Isaiah immediately has the full, exclusive right to run, build, and sell Orectic. (Belt and suspenders: the assignment-back and the spring-live license.)
  3. Isaiah is free to keep building and selling Orectic solo or with anyone else, with no ongoing obligation to the wound-down company.

The reversion is pre-agreed and self-executing — not something to be renegotiated in a bad moment. That's what makes it a real guarantee rather than a promise.

5. Trigger Events — and the critical carve-out

Reversion fires on a failure/unwind, never on success.

Triggers (reversion ON): - Dissolution, wind-down, or cessation of the company's operation of the product. - Insolvency / bankruptcy of the HoldCo. - A founder-deadlock or buy-sell separation that ends the partnership. - A mutually agreed unwind.

The carve-out (reversion OFF): - A bona-fide arm's-length sale or acquisition of the whole company. This is the win — the acquirer buys the HoldCo including Orectic, and Isaiah takes his (majority) share of the proceeds. Reversion must NOT fire on a sale, or it would poison every exit. Reversion is the parachute for failure, not a clause that scares off buyers. (Counsel will draft the change-of-control language so a real exit is cleanly excluded.)

5b. The mirror — Ovae stays with CT (this is symmetric)

Reversion isn't a one-way protection for Isaiah; it's a clean break where each founder keeps the lane they own. On the same Trigger Events:

  • Orectic reverts to Isaiah — the product and IP he built.
  • Ovae stays with CT — the brand, the go-to-market, the client relationships, and the team. And because the base is open-source, CT can rebuild the product layer with another technical partner — so he is no more stranded than Isaiah is.
  • The co-created moat is shared per §6; client relationships follow Ovae.

This symmetry is the point: each contributes their half into the company to build the upside together, and each walks away with their half — and a viable path forward — if it ends. That mutual exit-ramp is what makes it safe enough for both to fully commit now. (CT's protection here is the exact counterpart to Isaiah's reversion, and belongs in the same Contribution/Operating documents.)

6. The one nuance: the co-created moat on a split

The Orectic platform is unambiguously Isaiah's and reverts to him. The vertical data + runbooks + playbooks are different — CT and the team co-build them with every client, so a blanket "all of it goes to Isaiah" would unfairly strip the value CT created. Fair default on a split:

  • Each side gets a perpetual, non-exclusive license to the generalized, de-identified runbooks/playbooks developed during the partnership — so neither founder is crippled, and both can keep operating.
  • Client-specific data and relationships follow the client contracts (whoever continues serving a client carries that client's data, under the original consent terms).
  • Isaiah's reverted Orectic carries the platform's capabilities; it does not carry CT's client book.

This keeps the protection honest in both directions: Isaiah never loses the platform; CT never loses the moat he helped build.

7. Fairness guards (so neither side games it)

Reversion protects Isaiah from losing his work — it is not a hatch for walking off with the product opportunistically. Symmetric guards:

  • Isaiah can't trigger reversion by his own bad-faith exit. If Isaiah leaves without cause to grab the product, reversion is conditioned (e.g., it does not hand him the company's go-forward business, only the platform IP; and his unvested equity is still subject to the normal vesting/clawback in the founder agreement).
  • CT is protected because reversion (a) only fires on genuine unwind/failure, never on a sale, and (b) never sweeps in the co-created moat or the client book (§6), and (c) doesn't undo CT's vested equity or his share of any value realized before the unwind.
  • Creditors / third parties: counsel will structure the contribution + reversion so it isn't attackable as a fraudulent conveyance and survives a HoldCo bankruptcy (this is a real, technical point — flagged for the attorney).

8. How it's papered (the actual documents)

  1. Contribution & Assignment Agreement — Isaiah assigns the Orectic platform/IP/marks into Orectic LLC for his HoldCo equity; itemizes exactly what's contributed; sets the valuation/basis.
  2. Standby (Reversion) License Agreement — dormant, exclusive, springs live on a Trigger; defines triggers and the bona-fide-sale carve-out.
  3. Reversion / Spring-back Assignment — pre-signed, self-executing transfer of the Orectic sub/assets back to Isaiah on a Trigger.
  4. Operating / Shareholders' Agreement — houses the deadlock/buy-sell triggers, the fairness guards (§7), and ties reversion to the founder-departure scenarios.
  5. IP Assignment (all founders + contractors) — everyone assigns work product to the company so there are no orphan-IP gaps; consistent with §6 on the co-created moat.
  6. Client MSA data clauses — the de-identified data-rights + runbook-ownership terms that put the moat cleanly at the parent (so §6 is enforceable).

9. Why this works for both

  • Isaiah: he never loses what he built. Orectic powers the company and compounds — but if it all falls apart, the platform, the code, the brand, and the right to keep building come back to him, automatically. He gets the upside of building together and keeps the asset on the downside.
  • CT: he's not building on quicksand — reversion can't fire on a sale (the win is protected), can't sweep in the moat he co-built, and can't be used as an opportunistic exit hatch. The structure stays clean and investable.
  • The company: one entity to fund and one to acquire, with the IP inside it — premium-multiple-ready — and a downside parachute that makes the founder who contributed the crown jewel comfortable saying yes.

Open points for counsel / the two of you to decide

  1. HoldCo entity type (DE C-corp vs LLC) — drives §351 vs §721 and the sub structure. (Lean C-corp for venture optionality.)
  2. Exact trigger definitions — especially what counts as "founder deadlock" and the cure periods before reversion fires.
  3. Co-created-moat split (§6) — confirm the license-back default is fair, or define a cleaner division.
  4. Bad-faith-exit conditioning (§7) — how hard to make it, so it protects CT without making Isaiah feel his guarantee is hollow.
  5. Fraudulent-conveyance / bankruptcy-remoteness — a real technical structuring question for the attorney.
