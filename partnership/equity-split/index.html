The honest counter (Isaiah's 63/37) — and why it doesn't move the number

His legitimate points: he built it, he has the client, he has a real solo option, and your forward contribution isn't proven yet. All true. But 63/37 over-weights the day-one head start and ignores that Isaiah is already protected three separate ways — which is also your calm answer to "what have you actually delivered yet?":

  1. Vesting — you earn your 45% over time (a 4-yr/12-mo-cliff floor that accelerates on financing rounds, valuation milestones, and fully on a sale). It's not handed over for showing up.
  2. Reversion — if it doesn't work out, Orectic returns to him; he never loses what he built.
  3. The clean break — on a split he keeps Orectic and you keep Ovae; if you underdeliver and part, your unvested equity returns. Each keeps their lane.

A big equity premium on top of the reversion + your earn-in vesting is paying twice for risk Isaiah isn't bearing. His concern is real — and it's handled by structure, not by starting you at 37%. (Starting you sub-40% post-pool also quietly hurts the raise — investors read it as "employee with equity," per the VC lens.)

The number to hold

  • Present: 55% Isaiah / 45% CT + ~10–12% pool from both.
  • Defensible range: 52/48 → 58/42.
  • Floor: hold CT above 40% post-pool. Below that costs you on fundability and psychological ownership.
  • Don't open with a range — open with the framework, land on 55/45 as the conclusion.

How to present it — framework first, number last

  1. Get the vision out loud. "What does this look like in 5 years if we nail it?" Let Isaiah own the big number.
  2. Name the two engines. Deep product + distribution/capital. "A great OS with no distribution is a feature list; distribution with no OS is a pitch deck." Neither wins alone in this market.
  3. Acknowledge his leverage — explicitly. "You built it, you have the first client, you could keep going solo. I see that." This disarms the objection before it's played.
  4. Then name 55/45 — as the conclusion of shared logic, not an opening demand.
  5. Anchor the expanding pie (math, not rhetoric): "Solo, maybe a $5M outcome at 100% — that's $5M. Together: we raise, use my network to land 10 enterprise clients, position for $30M+ — your 55% is $16.5M. The question isn't what you give up; it's which bet you'd rather make."

Opening script (natural pace):

"Before numbers — I want to name something. You built this, you've got a paying client, you have real options. I'm not here to take a slice of that. I'm here to build something neither of us builds alone. The way I see it, this needs two things to become what we both think it can be: deep product and distribution at scale. You're the product; I'm the distribution. So here's where I landed: 55 you, 45 me, with a pool we carve out together before the raise. That reflects that you got here first and carry the technical risk — and it's enough for me to go all in, which is what this needs. What's your gut reaction?"

If he pushes back:

"I hear you — and I'd rather have this conversation now than have it blow up in year two. Tell me what feels off: is it the number, or is it not being sure what you'd be getting?"

Then listen. His answer tells you whether it's a math problem or a trust problem — they need different responses.

What this is NOT

  • Not a doc to hand Isaiah. Bring the conversation.
  • Not a case for why you "deserve" it — that's a zero-sum frame that triggers "I built it" defensiveness. Lead with what the company becomes, and let the split fall out of the logic.
  • Hold the split loosely and the partnership tightly. Signaling that the relationship matters more than the percentage is itself the move that gets you the percentage.
